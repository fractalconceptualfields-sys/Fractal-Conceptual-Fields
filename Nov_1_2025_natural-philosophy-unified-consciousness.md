## Part I: The Crisis of Philosophical Fragmentation

### Historical Context: The Collapse of Natural Philosophy

From Aristotle through the 17th century, **Natural Philosophy** unified the investigation of nature through inseparable inquiry into both mechanisms (efficient causation) and principles (final causation, telos, meaning). The trajectory of a stone involved both *how* (gravitational mechanics) and *why* (orientation toward its natural place).

**The Early 20th Century Divorce**

Logical positivism initiated systematic fragmentation:
- **Science** answers *how* questions (mechanistic causation, measurable facts)
- **Philosophy** addresses *why* questions (meaning, purpose, value)
- The assumption: these inquiries are categorically distinct

This artificial boundary produced:
- **Reductionism**: The belief that explaining mechanisms suffices to explain phenomena (they don't)
- **Emergentism**: The need to posit novel properties arising from mechanisms they don't actually follow from
- **Consciousness hard problem**: Inexplicable gap between physical substrate and phenomenal experience
- **Fragmented discourse**: Biology, physics, philosophy, consciousness studies proceed in mutual incomprehension

### The Recognition Deficit

Contemporary disciplines share data but not framework:
- **Neuroscience** demonstrates emotions bind experience (Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis) but treats binding as emergent property
- **Physics** describes constructal flow optimization but cannot explain why systems "choose" efficient forms
- **Philosophy** articulates consciousness problems but lacks empirical grounding for proposed solutions
- **Consciousness studies** accumulates metrics (integrated information, global workspace, predictive coding) but remains unable to explain why any physical system should generate subjective experience

**The fundamental error**: Treating *how* and *why* as separable categories rather than aspects of unified phenomenon.
